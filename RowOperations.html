Linear Algebra: Chapter 7 Summary

Matrices, Vectors, Determinants, and Linear Systems

1. Matrix Addition and Scalar Multiplication

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers. Addition is defined only for matrices of the same size $(m \times n)$, performed entry-wise: $[a_{jk}] + [b_{jk}] = [a_{jk} + b_{jk}]$. Scalar multiplication multiplies every entry by a constant $k$: $k[a_{jk}] = [ka_{jk}]$.

Practice Questions

1. Calculate $2A - B$ given $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: Multiply $A$ by scalar 2: $2A = \begin{bmatrix} 2(1) & 2(2) \\ 2(0) & 2(-1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 2: Subtract $B$ from $2A$ entry-wise: $\begin{bmatrix} 2-5 & 4-2 \\ 0-1 & -2-3 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 3: Simplify to get final answer: $\begin{bmatrix} -3 & 2 \\ -1 & -5 \end{bmatrix}$.

2. Let $C$ be a $3 \times 2$ matrix. If $3C + \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 \\ 3 & 7 \\ 8 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$, find $C$.

Step 1: Isolate $3C$ by subtracting the matrix from both sides: $3C = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 \\ 3 & 7 \\ 8 & 5 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 2: Perform subtraction: $3C = \begin{bmatrix} 4-1 & 3-0 \\ 3-0 & 7-1 \\ 8-2 & 5-2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 3 \\ 3 & 6 \\ 6 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 3: Multiply by scalar $\frac{1}{3}$: $C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

3. Deduction: Is it true that $(k+m)A = kA + mA$ for any scalar $k, m$ and matrix $A$? Prove it.

Step 1: Let $A = [a_{jk}]$. The left side is $(k+m)A = [(k+m)a_{jk}]$.

Step 2: Apply the distributive law for real numbers to the entries: $[ka_{jk} + ma_{jk}]$.

Step 3: By definition of matrix addition, split the matrix: $[ka_{jk}] + [ma_{jk}]$.

Step 4: By definition of scalar multiplication, this is $kA + mA$. Yes, it is true.

4. Proof: Show that matrix addition is commutative ($A + B = B + A$).

Step 1: Define $A = [a_{jk}]$ and $B = [b_{jk}]$.

Step 2: By definition, $A + B = [a_{jk} + b_{jk}]$.

Step 3: Since $a_{jk}$ and $b_{jk}$ are real numbers, and addition in $\mathbb{R}$ is commutative, $a_{jk} + b_{jk} = b_{jk} + a_{jk}$.

Step 4: Therefore, $[a_{jk} + b_{jk}] = [b_{jk} + a_{jk}] = B + A$.

5. Deduction: If $A+B=0$, prove $B = -1 \cdot A$.

Step 1: Start with matrix entries: $[a_{jk}] + [b_{jk}] = [0]$.

Step 2: This implies entry-wise $a_{jk} + b_{jk} = 0$ for all $j, k$.

Step 3: Rearranging gives $b_{jk} = -a_{jk}$.

Step 4: Rewrite $B$: $B = [b_{jk}] = [-a_{jk}] = -1 \cdot [a_{jk}] = -1 \cdot A$.

6. Compute the trace of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$ where $Tr(A) = \sum a_{ii}$.

Step 1: Identify the main diagonal entries where row index equals column index ($a_{11}$ and $a_{22}$).

Step 2: Here, $a_{11} = 4$ and $a_{22} = 3$.

Step 3: Sum the diagonal entries: $4 + 3 = 7$.

7. Find variables $x, y$ if $\begin{bmatrix} x+y \\ x-y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: Equate corresponding entries to form a system: $x+y=10$ and $x-y=2$.

Step 2: Add the two equations: $(x+y) + (x-y) = 10 + 2 \implies 2x = 12 \implies x=6$.

Step 3: Substitute $x=6$ into the first equation: $6+y=10 \implies y=4$.

8. Deduction: Prove that $k(A+B) = kA + kB$.

Step 1: Left side is $k([a_{jk}] + [b_{jk}]) = k[a_{jk} + b_{jk}]$.

Step 2: By scalar multiplication, this equals $[k(a_{jk} + b_{jk})]$.

Step 3: Apply distributive law for real numbers: $[ka_{jk} + kb_{jk}]$.

Step 4: Split into two matrices: $[ka_{jk}] + [kb_{jk}] = kA + kB$.

9. Deduction: If $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix, what are the dimensions of $k A^T$?

Step 1: The transpose operation $A^T$ swaps rows and columns.

Step 2: An $m \times n$ matrix becomes an $n \times m$ matrix.

Step 3: Scalar multiplication ($k$) does not change matrix dimensions. The dimensions remain $n \times m$.

10. Calculate $A + B$ if $A = [1, 2, 3]$ and $B = [4, 5, 6]$.

Step 1: Ensure matrices are same size. Both are $1 \times 3$ row vectors. Addition is valid.

Step 2: Add corresponding entries: $[1+4, 2+5, 3+6]$.

Step 3: Final matrix is $[5, 7, 9]$.

2. Rank and Linear Independence

The rank of a matrix $A$ is the maximum number of linearly independent row vectors (or column vectors). Vectors $v_1, \dots, v_k$ are linearly independent if $c_1v_1 + \dots + c_kv_k = 0$ implies all $c_i = 0$.

Practice Questions

1. Find the rank of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: Use row reduction. Notice that Row 2 is exactly 2 times Row 1.

Step 2: Apply operation $R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$. Matrix becomes $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 3: Swap $R_2$ and $R_3$ to get $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 4: There are 2 non-zero rows. Therefore, $rank(A) = 2$.

2. Are the vectors $v_1=[1, 0, 0], v_2=[0, 1, 0], v_3=[1, 1, 0]$ linearly independent?

Step 1: Set up the equation $c_1v_1 + c_2v_2 + c_3v_3 = \mathbf{0}$.

Step 2: Notice by observation that $v_3 = v_1 + v_2$.

Step 3: Therefore, $1(v_1) + 1(v_2) - 1(v_3) = \mathbf{0}$.

Step 4: We found non-zero coefficients ($c_1=1, c_2=1, c_3=-1$). They are linearly dependent.

3. Proof: Show that if a set of vectors contains the zero vector, it is linearly dependent.

Step 1: Let the set be $\{v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n\}$ where $v_1 = \mathbf{0}$.

Step 2: Consider the linear combination equation $c_1v_1 + c_2v_2 + \dots + c_nv_n = \mathbf{0}$.

Step 3: Choose $c_1 = 1$ (or any non-zero number) and $c_2 = c_3 = \dots = c_n = 0$.

Step 4: Then $1(\mathbf{0}) + 0(v_2) + \dots + 0 = \mathbf{0}$ is a valid solution where not all coefficients are zero.

Step 5: By definition, the set is linearly dependent.

4. Calculate the rank of a $4 \times 4$ identity matrix.

Step 1: The identity matrix $I_4$ is already in row-reduced echelon form.

Step 2: It has 1s on the main diagonal and 0s elsewhere.

Step 3: Therefore, there are exactly 4 non-zero rows.

Step 4: Rank is the number of non-zero rows, so $rank(I_4) = 4$.

5. Deduction: If $rank(A) = r$, what is the maximum possible value for $r$ if $A$ is $3 \times 5$?

Step 1: Rank represents the maximum number of linearly independent rows OR columns.

Step 2: The number of independent rows cannot exceed the total number of rows (3).

Step 3: The number of independent columns cannot exceed the total number of columns (5).

Step 4: Therefore, maximum rank $\le \min(3, 5)$. The maximum value is 3.

6. Proof: Prove $rank(A) = rank(A^T)$.

Step 1: By definition, $rank(A)$ is the maximum number of linearly independent row vectors in $A$ (Row Rank).

Step 2: The columns of $A^T$ are exactly the rows of $A$. So the maximum number of linearly independent columns in $A^T$ is $rank(A)$.

Step 3: By a fundamental theorem of linear algebra, row rank always equals column rank for any matrix.

Step 4: Thus, the column rank of $A^T$ is equal to its row rank, meaning $rank(A^T) = rank(A)$.

7. Determine if $v_1=[1,1]$ and $v_2=[1,-1]$ form a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Step 1: A basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$ requires 2 linearly independent vectors.

Step 2: Set up equation: $c_1[1,1] + c_2[1,-1] = [0,0]$.

Step 3: This gives system: $c_1+c_2=0$ and $c_1-c_2=0$. Adding them gives $2c_1 = 0 \implies c_1=0$, and thus $c_2=0$.

Step 4: Since the only solution is trivial, they are independent. Since there are 2 vectors in a 2D space, they form a basis.

8. Solve $Ax=0$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: Write as equations: $x_1 + x_2 = 0$ and $2x_1 + 2x_2 = 0$.

Step 2: The second equation is simply twice the first, so we only need $x_1 + x_2 = 0$.

Step 3: Solve for $x_1$ in terms of $x_2$: $x_1 = -x_2$.

Step 4: The general solution is vector $x = c[-1, 1]^T$ for any arbitrary scalar $c$.

9. Deduction: If $A$ is an $n \times n$ matrix and $rank(A) < n$, does $A^{-1}$ exist?

Step 1: If $rank(A) < n$, the rows of $A$ are not all linearly independent.

Step 2: By determinant properties, if rows are linearly dependent, $det(A) = 0$.

Step 3: An inverse $A^{-1}$ only exists if and only if $det(A) \neq 0$.

Step 4: Therefore, $A^{-1}$ does NOT exist. The matrix is singular.

10. Find $k$ such that $[1, k]$ and $[4, 8]$ are linearly dependent.

Step 1: For two vectors to be dependent, one must be a scalar multiple of the other.

Step 2: Let $c[1, k] = [4, 8]$.

Step 3: This means $c(1) = 4$ and $c(k) = 8$.

Step 4: From the first equation, $c = 4$. Substituting into the second, $4k = 8 \implies k = 2$.

3. Systems of Linear Equations

A system $Ax = b$ is consistent if $rank(A) = rank([A|b])$. Gauss Elimination uses row operations to transform the augmented matrix into row-echelon form to find solutions.

Practice Questions

1. Solve using Gauss: $x + y = 5$ and $x - y = 1$.

Step 1: Form augmented matrix: $\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 1 & 5 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 \end{array}\right]$.

Step 2: Perform $R_2 \to R_2 - R_1$ to eliminate $x$: $\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 1 & 5 \\ 0 & -2 & -4 \end{array}\right]$.

Step 3: Back-substitute: $-2y = -4 \implies y = 2$.

Step 4: Substitute $y$ into $R_1$: $x + 2 = 5 \implies x = 3$.

2. Determine the consistency of $x+y=2$ and $2x+2y=5$.

Step 1: Form augmented matrix: $\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 & 5 \end{array}\right]$.

Step 2: Perform $R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$: $\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right]$.

Step 3: The second row translates to $0x + 0y = 1$, which is impossible ($0=1$).

Step 4: Furthermore, $rank(A)=1 \neq rank([A|b])=2$. The system is inconsistent (no solutions).

3. Deduction: Show that a homogeneous system $Ax=0$ always has at least one solution.

Step 1: Consider the vector $x = \mathbf{0}$ (the vector where every entry is zero).

Step 2: Evaluate the matrix multiplication $A \cdot \mathbf{0}$. Every term in the resulting vector is a sum of products with zero.

Step 3: Thus, $A \cdot \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$ regardless of the entries in $A$.

Step 4: Because this holds true, $x=\mathbf{0}$ is always a solution (known as the trivial solution).

4. Solve: $x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 3$, $x_2 + x_3 = 2$, and $x_3 = 1$.

Step 1: The system is already in row-echelon form. Start from bottom (back substitution): $x_3 = 1$.

Step 2: Substitute $x_3$ into equation 2: $x_2 + 1 = 2 \implies x_2 = 1$.

Step 3: Substitute both into equation 1: $x_1 + 1 + 1 = 3 \implies x_1 = 1$.

Step 4: The unique solution is $x_1=1, x_2=1, x_3=1$.

5. Deduction: Under what condition does an $n \times n$ system $Ax=b$ have a unique solution?

Step 1: A system has a unique solution if there are no free variables after Gaussian elimination.

Step 2: This requires the rank of coefficient matrix $A$ to equal the number of unknowns ($n$).

Step 3: A square matrix has full rank if and only if its determinant is non-zero ($det(A) \neq 0$).

Step 4: Equivalently, the matrix $A$ must be invertible, allowing $x = A^{-1}b$.

6. Use Cramer's Rule to solve $2x+y=4$ and $x+2y=5$.

Step 1: Find the main determinant $D = det\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = 4 - 1 = 3$.

Step 2: Replace col 1 with constants to find $D_x = det\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 5 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = 8 - 5 = 3$.

Step 3: Replace col 2 with constants to find $D_y = det\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = 10 - 4 = 6$.

Step 4: Solve: $x = D_x/D = 3/3 = 1$ and $y = D_y/D = 6/3 = 2$.

7. Proof: If $x_1$ and $x_2$ are solutions to $Ax=0$, show $c_1x_1 + c_2x_2$ is also a solution.

Step 1: By the premise, we know $Ax_1 = 0$ and $Ax_2 = 0$.

Step 2: Substitute the linear combination into the system: $A(c_1x_1 + c_2x_2)$.

Step 3: Distribute matrix $A$: $= A(c_1x_1) + A(c_2x_2) = c_1(Ax_1) + c_2(Ax_2)$.

Step 4: Substitute the known values: $= c_1(0) + c_2(0) = 0$. Thus, it is a solution.

8. Deduction: Explain conceptually what an augmented matrix is.

Step 1: Consider a linear system $Ax=b$.

Step 2: To solve this simultaneously, we need the coefficients of the variables ($A$) and the constants on the right side ($b$).

Step 3: An augmented matrix $[A|b]$ simply appends vector $b$ as an extra column to $A$.

Step 4: This allows us to perform row operations on both sides of the equation simultaneously without writing the variables explicitly.

9. How many possible numbers of solutions can a linear system have? List them.

Step 1: A linear system involves intersecting lines/planes.

Step 2: They can have exactly 0 solutions (inconsistent, parallel lines).

Step 3: They can have exactly 1 solution (unique, single intersection point).

Step 4: They can have infinitely many solutions ($\infty$) (dependent, overlapping lines/planes).

10. Solve for variables $x, y, z$: $x+y+z=6$, $y+z=5$, and $z=3$.

Step 1: Start with the simplest equation at the bottom: $z = 3$.

Step 2: Substitute $z$ into the middle equation: $y + 3 = 5 \implies y = 2$.

Step 3: Substitute both $y$ and $z$ into the top equation: $x + 2 + 3 = 6 \implies x + 5 = 6$.

Step 4: Final solution: $x = 1$, $y = 2$, $z = 3$.

4. Determinants and Inverses

The determinant $det(A)$ is a scalar value that characterizes square matrices. $A^{-1}$ exists if and only if $det(A) \neq 0$. Formula for $2 \times 2$: $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix}$.

Practice Questions

1. Calculate $det \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: Identify elements: $a=3, b=4, c=1, d=2$.

Step 2: Use the determinant formula for $2 \times 2$ matrix: $ad - bc$.

Step 3: Multiply main diagonal entries: $3 \times 2 = 6$.

Step 4: Multiply off-diagonal entries: $4 \times 1 = 4$.

Step 5: Subtract the two values: $6 - 4 = 2$.

2. Find $A^{-1}$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: First calculate the determinant: $det(A) = (1)(4) - (2)(3) = 4 - 6 = -2$.

Step 2: Check if $det(A) \neq 0$. Yes, it's non-zero, so the inverse exists.

Step 3: Set up the formula: $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{-2} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$. Note the swapped main diagonal and negated off-diagonal elements.

Step 4: Multiply the scalar into the matrix: $\begin{bmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 1.5 & -0.5 \end{bmatrix}$.

3. Proof: Prove that $det(AB) = det(A)det(B)$ for generic $2 \times 2$ diagonal matrices.

Step 1: Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & 0 \\ 0 & a_2 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} b_1 & 0 \\ 0 & b_2 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 2: Individual determinants: $det(A) = a_1a_2 - 0 = a_1a_2$, and $det(B) = b_1b_2 - 0 = b_1b_2$.

Step 3: Matrix product: $AB = \begin{bmatrix} a_1b_1 & 0 \\ 0 & a_2b_2 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 4: Determinant of product: $det(AB) = (a_1b_1)(a_2b_2) - 0 = a_1b_1a_2b_2$.

Step 5: Rearrange terms: $a_1a_2b_1b_2 = det(A)det(B)$.

4. Deduction: Conceptually, what is the determinant of an upper triangular matrix?

Step 1: An upper triangular matrix has zeroes below its main diagonal.

Step 2: When calculating its determinant (e.g., using Laplace expansion down the first column), all terms become zero except the one containing $a_{11}$.

Step 3: Expanding further down the subsequent sub-matrices continues this pattern.

Step 4: Consequently, the determinant is simply the product of its main diagonal entries: $det(A) = a_{11} \cdot a_{22} \cdots a_{nn}$.

5. If $det(A) = 5$, find $det(A^T)$.

Step 1: By definition, the transpose $A^T$ is formed by turning the rows of $A$ into columns.

Step 2: The determinant of a square matrix remains invariant under the transpose operation because expansion by rows gives the same result as expansion by columns.

Step 3: Therefore, $det(A^T) = det(A)$.

Step 4: The answer is 5.

6. Solve for $x$: $det \begin{bmatrix} x & 1 \\ 4 & x \end{bmatrix} = 0$.

Step 1: Expand the determinant expression using the $2 \times 2$ formula $ad-bc$.

Step 2: The equation becomes $(x)(x) - (1)(4) = 0$.

Step 3: Simplify: $x^2 - 4 = 0$.

Step 4: Add 4 to both sides: $x^2 = 4$.

Step 5: Take the square root: $x = \pm 2$.

7. Deduction: Show that $(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$.

Step 1: By definition, a matrix $X$ is the inverse of $B$ if $XB = BX = I$.

Step 2: Let's find the inverse of the matrix $B = A^{-1}$.

Step 3: We know from properties of inverses that $A(A^{-1}) = (A^{-1})A = I$.

Step 4: This equation exactly fits the definition of $A$ being the inverse of $A^{-1}$.

Step 5: Therefore, the inverse of $A^{-1}$, written as $(A^{-1})^{-1}$, is $A$.

8. Find the minor $M_{11}$ of the generic $3 \times 3$ matrix $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: The minor $M_{ij}$ is the determinant of the submatrix obtained by deleting row $i$ and column $j$.

Step 2: To find $M_{11}$, delete Row 1 and Column 1.

Step 3: The remaining $2 \times 2$ submatrix is $\begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 4: Calculate its determinant: $(5)(9) - (6)(8) = 45 - 48 = -3$. Thus, $M_{11} = -3$.

9. Deduction: If $det(A) = 0$, what can you deduce about the rows of $A$?

Step 1: The determinant of an $n \times n$ matrix corresponds to the volume of the $n$-dimensional parallelepiped spanned by its row vectors.

Step 2: A determinant of 0 means the volume is "flattened" into a lower dimension.

Step 3: This occurs if and only if one row can be expressed as a linear combination of the other rows.

Step 4: Therefore, the rows of matrix $A$ must be linearly dependent.

10. Calculate $A^2$ if $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: Identify that $A$ is the $2 \times 2$ identity matrix, $I_2$.

Step 2: $A^2$ means multiplying the matrix by itself: $A \times A$.

Step 3: Substituting $I$: $I_2 \times I_2$.

Step 4: A fundamental property of the identity matrix is that multiplying any matrix $M$ by $I$ yields $M$. Applying this to itself means $I \times I = I$.

Step 5: Thus, $A^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.
